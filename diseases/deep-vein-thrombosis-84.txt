DEEP VEIN THROMBOSIS
Deep vein thrombosisAlso known as DVT,  Blood clot in the leg, or Thromboembolism
OVERVIEW
Overview
Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) is a condition in which a blood clot forms in a deep vein, most commonly in the legs, typically in the thigh or lower leg. DVT can occur due to prolonged immobility, like long flights or bed rest, surgery, injury to veins, pregnancy, certain medications (like hormone therapy), or medical conditions like cancer.


Though some people may have no symptoms, swelling, pain, or tenderness (often in one leg), warmth, and redness in the affected area are some of the common symptoms of DVT. Risk factors include being over 60,obesity,smoking, a family history of blood clots, or having a clotting disorder.



These clots can partially or completely block blood flow, leading to pain and swelling. If the clot breaks free, it can travel to the lungs and cause a life-threatening complication calledpulmonary embolism(PE). Treatment involves anticoagulant (blood-thinning) medications, compression stockings, and lifestyle modifications to reduce the recurrence of symptoms.

CAUSES OF DEEP VEIN THROMBOSIS
Causes Of Deep Vein Thrombosis

DVT happens when a blood clot forms in a deep vein, usually in the legs. This process is driven by three key things, often called Virchow’s Triad:

Slow or reduced blood flow:If you’re sitting or lying down for a long time, like during long flights, hospital stays, or after surgery, your blood doesn’t move as actively through your legs. This creates a perfect setting for clots to form.

Damage to the blood vessel lining:If the inner lining of your veins gets injured from surgery, trauma, or a catheter, your body sees it as damage and starts forming a clot as a “repair patch.”

Increased clotting tendency:Some people’s blood clots more easily due to conditions like cancer, pregnancy, genetic disorders, or certain medications (like birth control pills). This means clots can form even without much injury.

DIAGNOSIS OF DEEP VEIN THROMBOSIS
Diagnosis Of Deep Vein Thrombosis

Early and accurate diagnosis of DVT is essential to prevent serious complications like pulmonary embolism. Doctors typically use a combination of clinical evaluation and diagnostic tests to confirm the presence of a clot. These include:

1. CLINICAL ASSESSMENT (WELLS SCORE)
1. Clinical assessment (Wells score)

Doctors use a scoring system based on your symptoms (like leg swelling, recent surgery, or cancer) to guess the likelihood of deep vein thrombosis.
It helps them decide if further testing is needed right away or not.

2.D-DIMERBLOOD TEST
2.D-dimerblood test

This test looks for a substance in your blood that forms when clots break down.
A low level often rules out DVT.

3. PLETHYSMOGRAPHY (LEG VOLUME TEST)
3. Plethysmography (leg volume test)

This group of tests checks how blood flows in your leg by measuring small changes in leg size or blood flow.

Digital photoplethysmography: It uses light sensors on your skin to track how quickly veins refill. A fast refill is normal; a slow refill may mean DVT.

Strain gauge plethysmography:Measures how quickly blood drains from your leg after brief pressure. Slow drainage may signal a blockage.

Impedance plethysmography:Uses electric signals to monitor blood flow.

4. IMAGING TESTS
4. Imaging tests

Compression ultrasound:This is the most commonly used test. A handheld device presses gently on your leg veins. If the vein doesn’t compress, there may be a clot.

Duplex andcolor doppler ultrasound:These advanced ultrasound scans also check how blood is flowing through your veins using sound waves and color.

Serial ultrasound:If your first scan is negative but you're still at risk, doctors may repeat the scan after a few days. This helps catch clots that may have been too small to detect earlier.

Venography:A dye is injected into your vein, and X-rays are taken to spot clots. It’s very accurate, but not commonly done because it's invasive and can cause allergic reactions.

CT orMRI scans:Used in special cases or when ultrasound is unclear. They give a more detailed view of deeper or hard-to-reach veins, especially in the pelvis or abdomen.

TREATMENT OF DEEP VEIN THROMBOSIS
Treatment Of Deep Vein Thrombosis

Treatment for DVT may vary depending on the individual risk of forming new clots. It consists of the following:

ANTICOAGULATION (BLOOD THINNERS)
Anticoagulation (blood thinners)

These stop existing clots from growing and help prevent new ones. They can be taken as pills, injections, or IV. Commonly used drugs are:HeparinWarfarin

NEWER ORAL BLOOD THINNERS
Newer oral blood thinners

TheseDirect Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)offer more convenience than older drugs like warfarin. They work by directly targeting specific clotting factors (thrombin or Factor Xa). Examples include:Dabigatran etexilateRivaroxabanApixabanEdoxaban

THROMBOLYSIS OR CLOT-BUSTING THERAPY
Thrombolysis or clot-busting therapy

This therapy is used for large or limb-threatening clots, typically in hospitals. These therapies carry a higher risk of bleeding and are reserved for select patients.

INFERIOR VENA CAVA (IVC) FILTERS
Inferior Vena Cava (IVC) filters

These are small, cage-like devices placed in the inferior vena cava (a large vein in the abdomen). They’re used when blood thinners can’t be given to catch and stop large clots from traveling to the lungs, helping prevent a pulmonary embolism (PE).

COMPLICATIONS OF DEEP VEIN THROMBOSIS
Complications Of Deep Vein Thrombosis

While DVT can often be managed well if caught early, it may lead to serious complications, like:

ACUTE COMPLICATIONS
Acute complications

Pulmonary embolism(PE):A piece of the clot travels to the lungs and blocks blood flow. It can cause breathlessness, chest pain, or sudden collapse.

Phlegmasia alba dolens:It is a large clot obstructing deep veins, which can cause sudden leg swelling and pain with pale skin.

Phlegmasia cerulea dolens:It is a severe form with a bluish leg, intense pain, and risk of tissue damage from lack of circulation.

Venous gangrene:If blood flow is completely blocked, the leg tissue may start to die. It can lead to amputation if not treated quickly.

CHRONIC COMPLICATIONS
Chronic complications

Post-thrombotic syndrome (PTS):It is long-term leg pain, swelling, and heaviness due to damaged veins after DVT.

Chronic venous insufficiency:Poor vein function causes fluid buildup, skin changes, and sometimes ulcers.

Recurrent DVT:Once you've had one DVT, your risk of having another increases, especially without preventive care.

ALTERNATIVE THERAPIES FOR DEEP VEIN THROMBOSIS
Alternative Therapies For Deep Vein Thrombosis

Alternative therapies can offer relief from symptoms of DVT, such as swelling or discomfort. However, they must not replace medical treatments like. Always consult your doctor before starting anything new. Therapies that can help include:

TRADITIONAL CHINESE MEDICINE (TCM)
Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)
TCM may include herbal formulas and cupping to promote circulation and reduce inflammation. Some herbs may interact with blood thinners, so it is always advisable to talk to your doctor first.

YOGA
Yoga
Gentle yoga postures can support blood flow in the legs, ease stiffness, and reduce pain. Strenuous poses should be avoided if you have an active DVT.

ACUPUNCTURE
Acupuncture
Acupuncture involves needle pricking at different pressure points that may help relieve leg pain and support vascular health. However, it should be performed by a trained professional with awareness of clotting risks to avoid complications.

AROMATHERAPY
Aromatherapy
Essential oils like lavender or peppermint may ease discomfort when used in foot soaks or light massage. It’s more helpful for relaxation than direct DVT treatment. Remember to avoid messaging the affected leg.

LIVING WITH DEEP VEIN THROMBOSIS
Living With Deep Vein Thrombosis

Living with Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) means adapting your lifestyle to manage symptoms, prevent complications, and reduce the risk of recurrence. With the right care and awareness, most people can lead full, active lives. Here are some tips that can help:

1. BE MINDFULLY ACTIVE
1. Be mindfully active

Stay active with gentle exercises like walking or leg movements help keep blood flowing
Elevate your legs a few times a day to reduce swelling
Avoid sitting/standing too long and move every 30–60 minutes, especially on long journeys.

2. STICK TO PRESCRIBED MEDICATIONS
2. Stick to prescribed medications

Take your medications exactly as prescribed by your doctor
Don’t skip doses, and follow up regularly to monitor blood levels
Let your other doctors know that you're taking anticoagulants, so they can avoid prescribing medications or procedures that may interact or increase your risk of bleeding.

3. DON’T SKIP COMPRESSION STALKINGS
3. Don’t skip compression stalkings

Wear doctor-recommended compression stockings, as they can relieve swelling and pain
Use them during the day and remove them during the night, or as suggested by your doctor.

4. FOCUS ON DIET AND HYDRATION
4. Focus on diet and hydration

Stay well hydrated, as it helps prevent clot formation
Maintain a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains for overall health
